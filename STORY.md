# The Placard

The first time I edited a website, I didn't know what a website was.

I was nine or ten, at my friend Justin's house, on his dad's computer. Justin had discovered something: if you right-clicked on a page and saved it, you got a file. And if you opened that file, you could change it. We spent an afternoon editing the MSN homepage. We had no idea what we were doing. We didn't know if we were changing it for everyone, or just for the screen in front of us. We didn't really understand the difference. We were just doing it, and it felt enormous.

---

A few years later, when my family finally got a computer of our own, I found Geocities. This was before you found things online by searching for words — that was something Google hadn't invented yet. Seriously. You navigated by category. Yahoo had a catalog of pages, organized into smaller and smaller sub-categories, and you browsed through them like a library. Everyone who had a webpage had a Geocities account. It wasn't the internet yet the way we know it now. It was smaller. Handmade. You could tell a person had made each page.

Around the same time, I found GameFAQs. It started as a single webpage where one person named Jeff collected text files: guides other people had written on how to beat video games. Then that person wrote message boards — code from scratch — so people could talk to each other on his website. Then he made a board for every game that existed. I was user #1694. My username was just "Steve." The internet was that small. You could still be early to it.

One person was doing all of this. One person was making something that mattered to everyone who played games.

---

Then came Million Dollar Homepage. One person. One page. A grid of pixels for sale to anyone with a credit card and something to say. Before MySpace. Before Facebook. Back when the internet was still the wild west and an insane idea could just work. It wasn't really about the money, even though the money was the hook. It was about the mark. One person got to put their mark on the world, and then a thousand other people paid to put theirs right next to it. The audacity of it. The simplicity of it.

---

Then the internet got big.

Then it got smooth. Then it got owned. Three or four companies absorbed most of it, and the rest of us became users of platforms instead of makers of pages. The idea that one person could still make a dent, that started to feel naïve. Building something real online required money, or a team, or skills that took years to develop. 

---

Then I tried something.

I had an idea for the dumbest possible website. It wasn't a product or a startup or a portfolio piece. It was a joke — a single page for a single moment, the moment when someone says *"like 6... 7."* You know the one.

I opened a chat with an AI and described what I wanted. I didn't write a line of code. The AI wrote the whole thing — the HTML, the animations, the icons, the install prompt for your phone. When something was wrong, I described what was wrong. When I wanted something new, I described what I wanted. The whole site, from nothing to live at like67.com, in a single afternoon.

---

That's what changed.

Not that AI writes code. That doesn't really matter. What matters is that the craziness is back — the good kind. The kind where you're nine years old and you think you can just edit the internet, and maybe you're right.

The leveling of the playing field isn't a metaphor. It's a chat window. Your idea is enough to start.
